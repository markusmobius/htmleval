 Guests staying at the Alpine Hotel Kristiania in Lech, Austria, can enjoy creative fine dining at the hotel’s Otto Wagner Schützenhaus.  While many people know goulash as being traditionally Hungarian, it has spread beyond borders to neighboring countries like Austria. A stew of meat and vegetables, seasoned with paprika and other spices, there’s nothing more warming than a big pot of goulash. Find it in Vienna in the Innere Stadt district’s Gulasch & Champagne. Robert Osmark, Vienna Tourist Board

 One of the best-known sweets of Viennese cuisine, after the Sacher Tort, is Kaiserschmarren. It’s made from shredded pancake dough and topped with raisins and sugar, and is the perfect treat on a cold day. In Vienna, you can find it at the famous confectionary and coffee shop, Demel. Robert Osmark, Vienna Tourist Board

 While Vanillekipferl (crescent cookies originating from Vienna, Austria) are a tradition around Christmastime, they are so popular that they can be found throughout the winter in Viennese coffee shops. They’re made from flour, butter and ground hazelnuts or almonds and are sprinkled on top with heaps of vanilla sugar. Harald Eisenberger, Austrian Tourist Office

 Maultaschen are meat-filled pasta squares from the Baden-Württemberg state in Southwest Germany. Legend has it that the monks of the Maulbronn Monastery were unwilling to go without meat during Lent, so they concealed the forbidden food in a pasta dough. You can find them in various restaurants in Baden-Württemberg’s capital Stuttgart, like the aptly named Roland’s Maultaschen and Maultaschenhäusle. Jean-Claude Winkler, courtesy of Stuttgart-Marketing GmbH

 In cold weather, sometimes all you need is a steak. For this reason, a favorite dish in the Swabian region of Austria and Southwest Germany is Zwiebelrostbraten — a steak with fried onions that often comes with spätzle, a regional type of noodle. An excellent version can be found at Zur Weinsteige inside the Hotel Wörtz in Stuttgart, Germany, a restaurant that specializes in traditional Swabian cuisine. Jean-Claude Winkler, courtesy of Stuttgart-Marketing GmbH

 Finnish people know a thing or two about staying warm — temperatures can reach as low as -7 degrees Fahrenheit in the Scandinavian country. A popular way to warm up here is by slurping salmon soup, made with butter, leek, carrots, cubed potatoes, salmon fillet cut into small chunks, fresh dill, salt and pepper. You can find it at the venerable restaurant Elite in Helsinki, Finland, which has been open since 1932. Julia Kivela, Visit Finland

 Svickova is a classic Czech dish made for weddings or Sunday family lunches, and every family seems to have their own version. But everybody can agree that the vegetable sauce with cream contains carrots, celery and parsley, that it involves a piece of beef, and that it is served with bread dumplings. A great version can be had at Na Pekarne in the Cakovicky village just outside of Prague. courtesy of Czech Tourism

 Crepes Suzette, which is rumored to have been created at the Cafe de Paris in Monaco in 1896 and is still served here. The dish consists of crepes with Grand Marnier and orange juice that are often set ablaze tableside. Legend has it that chef Henri Charpentier had the idea of serving the combination to the future King of England, Edward VII, when they inadvertently caught fire. He served them to the Prince who named them after his young guest, Suzette. Michael Alesi

 An archipelago that’s 1,000 miles off the coast of Europe and an autonomous region of Portugal, The Azores offers an incredibly diverse and stunning environment, including volcanic terrain. On the island of Sao Miguel in the Furnas Valley, you can find volcano-cooked Cozido das Furnas stew. The stew is cooked by placing a pot of vegetables and meat into the ground, which is then heated by volcanic steam. courtesy of AzoresGetaways.com
